Reflection

My Journey in Software Development

When I first started writing code, I had no idea what I was getting myself into. It started with a simple Python script to automate a task I found tedious, and before I knew it, I was spending weekends diving deeper into programming concepts just because I found it genuinely enjoyable. That curiosity has been the driving force behind everything I've built since — not assignments or deadlines, but the simple satisfaction of making something work.

Over time, that curiosity evolved into something more deliberate. Working at Socket Fiber gave me my first real taste of production software, where mistakes have consequences and code quality actually matters. Writing and maintaining CalendarBuddy — a tool used by real people doing real work — changed how I think about software. It's no longer just about making the program run; it's about making it reliable, maintainable, and easy for others to use.

My studies at Mizzou have filled in the formal gaps: data structures, systems design, security principles, and the kind of theoretical foundation that helps you understand why things work the way they do. But if I'm honest, some of my most valuable lessons have come from debugging production issues at 11 PM, reading documentation for libraries nobody else was using, or just breaking things in my homelab and figuring out how to fix them. Failure is a surprisingly good teacher when you're stubborn enough to push through it.

Looking ahead, I'm excited about the intersection of automation and infrastructure — building systems that can reason about themselves and adapt without constant human intervention. Whether that's intelligent monitoring for critical systems or smarter tooling for development workflows, I want to work on problems that are genuinely hard and genuinely matter. This portfolio is a snapshot of where I am right now, and I hope it reflects not just what I've built, but how I think.
